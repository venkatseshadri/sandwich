Brain runs maybe 5–30 times a day, only on candidate setups

Option C — Fixed cadence (3–5 min)
Middle ground but worst of both worlds — slow enough to miss fast moves,

Option D — On-demand
You manually press a button. Useful for backtesting and learning the system, but

Pure LLM pattern recognition on raw data: sounds magical, doesn't work well. LLMs

The post-mortem researcher's job: look at every paper trade, look at all the data around
it, and reverse-engineer what would have predicted the outcome.
This is fundamentally a different problem from the live entry-researcher I sketched. It's
closer to feature discovery / hypothesis generation than real-time decision making.

Why this is actually a brilliant setup
Most retail traders skip this step and jump straight to "let me code an EMA crossover
strategy." You're doing it correctly:

✓

1. Generate unbiased samples (random-time entries) you have this
2. Mine the data for what retrospectively explains winners vs losers ← this researcher
3. Form hypotheses about edge ("5 EMA break + hourly green + 15m yellow seems to
precede 25% moves")
4. Validate hypotheses on held-out data
5. Only then deploy as live entry rules
Steps 3 and 4 are where most systems fail. Let's design for that.

The honest design problem
If you just dump trade outcomes + all surrounding data into an LLM and ask "what
predicted this?", you'll get confident-sounding pattern-matching garbage. LLMs are
extremely prone to post-hoc rationalization on numeric data. They'll find "patterns" in
12 trades that don't generalize to trade 13.

The indicator problem — why "more is worse" until you organize them
If you feed 40 indicators into a pattern miner, you will find "patterns" that are pure noise.
With 40 features and 250 trades, statistical theory says you'll find spurious correlations
with p<0.05 in roughly 2 features purely by chance. The LLM will then write convincing

The fix isn't to throw indicators away — it's to organize them into orthogonal families
and treat each family as one concept with one representative signal, not five
overlapping ones.

The seven indicator families (this is the mental model to adopt)
Every indicator you have falls into exactly one of these. I'll give you the principle, then
concrete examples, then which one to actually use.
1. Trend / Direction (lagging by nature) What it measures: is the underlying going up,
down, or sideways, on a given timeframe. Common members: EMA (5, 9, 20, 50, 200),
SMA, Supertrend, ADX direction, Ichimoku cloud, HMA, linear regression slope. The
trap: EMA 5, EMA 9, and EMA 20 are ~85% correlated on 1-min data. Stacking them is
redundant. Pick one per timeframe: I'd suggest EMA 20 + EMA 50 (fast and slow
context) per TF, or Supertrend if you prefer a single binary trend bit. Stop there.
2. Momentum / Rate of change (leading-ish, oscillator family) What it measures: how
fast price is changing, and whether it's overextended. Common members: RSI,
Stochastic, Stoch RSI, MACD histogram, ROC, Williams %R, CCI. The trap: RSI and
Stochastic and Williams %R are nearly the same thing on different scales. MACD
histogram is just an EMA-of-EMA difference — it's a trend indicator dressed up as
momentum. Pick one per timeframe: RSI(14) is fine. Or Stoch RSI if you want faster
signals. Don't carry both.
3. Volatility / Range expansion What it measures: how much room the price is moving
in, regardless of direction. Common members: ATR, Bollinger Band width, Keltner
Channel width, standard deviation, historical volatility. The trap: All four measure the
same underlying thing. Pick one: ATR(14) plus ATR percentile (where current ATR sits
in last 20 days). Percentile matters more than raw value — it tells you "are we in a high or
low volatility regime?"
4. Volume / Participation What it measures: how much conviction is behind the move.
Common members: raw volume, VWAP, volume MA, OBV, MFI, A/D line, CVD
(cumulative volume delta). The trap: For Nifty/BankNifty index options, spot volume is
less meaningful than for stocks. What matters more is futures volume and option-side
participation (which lives in the options family below). Pick: VWAP (anchor for
intraday mean reversion / trend confirmation), and volume vs 20-period average as a
ratio. That's it.
5. Option chain / Positioning (this is huge for you, and most retail systems ignore it)
What it measures: where smart money is positioned. Common members: Put-Call Ratio
(volume PCR vs OI PCR), Max Pain, OI buildup at strikes, OI change vs price change
(long buildup / short buildup / long unwinding / short covering), IV percentile, IV skew,
ATM IV vs realized vol. The trap: PCR alone is noisy. Max pain alone is overrated near
expiry. OI without price-context is meaningless. Pick the matrix: OI change × Price
change is the gold standard — it tells you whether the move is real (long buildup) or

Leading vs lagging — be honest about this
There is no such thing as a purely leading indicator on price data. Every indicator
computed from past prices is, by definition, lagging the price itself. What people mean
when they say "leading" is one of two things:
1. Faster-reacting lagging indicators (Stoch reacts faster than MACD because it has
shorter lookback) — still lagging, just less so.
2. Indicators from a different data source that has predictive power for price (option
OI, FII flows, VIX, overnight US markets, GIFT Nifty premium) — these can
genuinely lead price because they reflect information not yet absorbed.

Short answer: iterate small, one layer at a time. You absolutely do not need all four
layers to start. In fact, building all four upfront would be a mistake.
Here's why, and what the iteration path should look like.

Why "all four layers first" is the wrong instinct
Each layer's design depends on what you learn from the layer below it. If you build all
four now:
Layer 2's pattern miner gets configured based on assumptions about feature
distributions you haven't seen yet
Layer 3's bull/bear agents get prompts written for patterns you haven't actually
found yet
Layer 4's librarian gets a schema for hypotheses you don't yet know the shape of
You'd be writing 80% speculative code. When real data hits, half of it gets rewritten.
The other failure mode: building all four and then discovering the indicator itself
doesn't have edge. You've spent weeks of work to validate a hypothesis you could've
killed in 2 days.

The iteration ladder
Each rung is a self-contained experiment that gives you a real answer before you
commit to the next rung.
Rung 1 — One indicator, one timeframe, manual analysis (no LLM, no agents)

distribution. Plot it.

losers? If yes → continue. If no → try a different indicator, don't add more layers.
Time: ~1 day.
Rung 2 — Two or three features, still manual

Yes — and this is a good question because it surfaces something we should be explicit
about before going further. Let me unpack it carefully, because there's a right way and a
wrong way to fold patterns into Sandwich.

The short answer
Yes, Sandwich can use pullback-from-reversal patterns. But not the way most traders
imagine. Patterns enter Sandwich as features, not as rules, and they earn their place by
lifting the base probability of the labels you've already defined — not by being
intrinsically "right."

The longer answer — why the framing matters
There are two completely different ways to use patterns in a trading system, and they
have wildly different success rates.
The wrong way: pattern → trade "If I see a pullback from a reversal, enter long." This is
how most retail systems use patterns. It fails for two reasons. First, "pullback from
reversal" isn't a single thing — it has many variants, fuzzy boundaries, and arguments
about what counts. Second, even when the pattern is unambiguously present, its
forward edge is usually small, regime-dependent, and easily destroyed by transaction
costs.
The right way: pattern → feature → label probability shift "When the pullback-fromreversal pattern is present, P(won't_crash_60min_25pts) historically rises from 0.55 to
0.68." This is how patterns should enter Sandwich. The pattern doesn't decide the trade.
It updates the probability that the base label condition will hold. The trade decision
happens downstream, the same way it does for any other feature.
This matters because the exact same pattern might have lift on won't_crash_30min
but no lift on won't_crash_session , or might have lift only when VIX is below 14. You
only discover this by treating patterns as features competing for statistical relevance,
not as standalone trading rules.

The honest caveats
A few things I want to be straight with you about, because the topic of "patterns"
attracts a lot of magical thinking:
1. Most discretionary patterns disappear under proper statistical scrutiny. Backtest a

small. This is why Sandwich treats them as one feature among many, not as the

pattern" can have different microstructure (volume, time-of-day, prior trend strength,
VIX regime). A pattern's edge often lives in its interaction with non-pattern features,

3. The biggest pattern win is usually as a kill flag, not as a buy signal. "We just had a
sharp reversal on the higher TF" might not generate a profitable long signal, but it might
kill a short signal that would otherwise fire. Patterns are often more useful in vetoing
than in initiating.
4. SMC concepts, if you use them, need precise definitions before they're useful in a
system. "Liquidity sweep" sounds great in a YouTube video. Defined as "wick beyond
prior K-bar high by ≥M points followed by close back inside, within N minutes" — now
it's a feature. Anyone uncomfortable with that level of precision shouldn't include the
feature.
